Interpreting a Cluster Randomized Controlled Trial: Digital Intervention for Type 2 Diabetes Prevention

While learning how to critically interpret randomized controlled trials (RCTs), I analyzed a research protocol that evaluates a digital lifestyle intervention designed to prevent type 2 diabetes among individuals with prediabetes in India. Type 2 diabetes is a rapidly growing health concern in India, and preventive strategies that can reach large populations are increasingly important. Digital health interventions delivered through smartphones may provide scalable and cost-effective solutions, especially in settings where traditional in-person programs are difficult to implement.

Study Snapshot

  • Study Design: Cluster Randomized Controlled Trial
  • Participants: 3,240 individuals with prediabetes
  • Location: Delhi and Tamil Nadu, India
  • Intervention: Digital lifestyle program delivered through a mobile application
  • Control Group: Standard lifestyle advice and educational materials
  • Follow-up Duration: 24 months
  • Primary Outcome: Incidence of Type 2 Diabetes

Study Objective

The objective of this trial is to determine whether a digital lifestyle intervention delivered through a mobile application can reduce the incidence of type 2 diabetes among adults with prediabetes.

Study Design

This study is designed as a cluster randomized controlled trial. Instead of randomizing individual participants, entire clusters such as urban wards and rural villages are randomized to either the intervention group or the control group. A total of 3,240 participants with prediabetes will be recruited from Delhi and Tamil Nadu. Participants will be followed for 24 months to evaluate whether the digital intervention can prevent progression to type 2 diabetes. Cluster randomization helps reduce contamination between participants living in the same community who might influence each other's behaviors.

Participants

Inclusion Criteria

Participants eligible for the study include:

  • Adults aged 18–60 years
  • Individuals with prediabetes
  • Participants with an Indian Diabetes Risk Score (IDRS) ≥ 30
  • Individuals who own and can use a smartphone

Exclusion Criteria

Participants are excluded if they:

  • Already have diagnosed diabetes
  • Are pregnant
  • Have conditions affecting metabolism or body weight
  • Have previously undergone bariatric surgery

These criteria ensure that the study focuses on individuals at risk of developing diabetes but who may benefit from preventive interventions.

Intervention vs Control

Intervention Group

Participants in the intervention group receive access to a mobile application called “My Sugar Journey.”

The app provides several features to support lifestyle modification, including:

  • Nutrition guidance
  • Physical activity tracking
  • Weight monitoring
  • Behavioral coaching through an AI chatbot
  • Educational health content
  • Gamification elements to encourage engagement

Participants may also receive reminder calls if they become inactive on the platform.

Control Group

Participants in the control group receive standard lifestyle advice and educational materials related to diabetes prevention but do not receive access to the digital intervention.

Planned Outcomes

Since this paper is a study protocol, the results of the trial have not yet been reported. Instead, the paper describes the outcomes that will be measured during the study.

Primary Outcome

The primary outcome of the trial is the incidence of type 2 diabetes after 24 months of follow-up.

Secondary Outcomes

The study will also evaluate several secondary outcomes, including:

  • Changes in HbA1c levels
  • Changes in fasting glucose and oral glucose tolerance test results
  • Changes in body weight and blood pressure
  • Improvements in lifestyle behaviors
  • Participant engagement with the digital intervention
  • Cost-effectiveness of the intervention

Strengths and Limitations

Strengths

This study has several important strengths:

  • Large sample size of over 3,000 participants
  • Inclusion of both urban and rural populations
  • Cluster randomized design, which reduces contamination between participants
  • Use of digital health technology with potential for large-scale implementation
  • Long 24-month follow-up period

Limitations

  • Differences in digital literacy may affect participant engagement with the mobile application
  • Reliance on smartphone access, which may vary across populations
  • Behavioral interventions often depend on participant motivation and adherence

Reference
