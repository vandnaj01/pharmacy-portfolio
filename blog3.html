Monitoring Assessment of a Randomized Clinical Trial on Maternal Iron Deficiency Anemia

Study Overview

Study Title: Single-dose intravenous iron vs oral iron for treatment of maternal iron deficiency anemia: a randomized clinical trial

Trial Name: RAPID IRON Trial

Registration Number: CTRI/2020/09/027730 (Clinical Trials Registry of India)

Phase: Not specified in the publication (comparative randomized clinical trial evaluating already approved therapies)

Therapeutic Area: Maternal Health / Obstetrics

Protocol & Methodology Review

Primary Objective:
The primary objective of this randomized clinical trial was to evaluate whether a single dose of intravenous iron therapy is superior to twice-daily oral iron supplementation in pregnant women with moderate iron-deficiency anemia. The trial specifically evaluated two main outcomes: Reduction in the incidence of low birth weight infants (<2500 g) Achievement of maternal non-anemic status (hemoglobin ≥11 g/dL) during pregnancy.

The study compared three treatment groups:

  • Oral ferrous sulfate- Comparator Agent Dose- 60 mg of elemental iron tablets Frequency-Two tablets a day if Hb <11.0, and once daily if Hb 11.0 along with prophylactic dose of antihelminthic therapy(albendazole) in the second trimester. Route of administration-Oral Duration of therapy-from 14-17 weeks of pregnancy until delivery
  • Intravenous ferric Derisomaltose- Intervention Dose-up to 1 g Frequency-Single dose Route of administration-Intravenous Duration of therapy-A single dose administered during 14-17 weeks of pregnancy
  • Intravenous ferric carboxymaltose- Intervention Dose-up to 1 g Frequency-Single dose Route of administration-Intravenous Duration of therapy-A single dose administered during 14-17 weeks of pregnancy

Participants were randomly assigned in a 1:1:1 ratio.

Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria:
Inclusion: Eligible participants were pregnant women between 14 and 17 weeks of gestation with moderate iron-deficiency anemia (hemoglobin 7.0–9.9 g/dL) and singleton pregnancy. These criteria are clinically appropriate because treatment during the second trimester allows sufficient time for correction of anemia before delivery.

Exclusion: Fetal anomalies, cardiovascular disease, known hemoglobinopathy, or hypersensitivity to IV iron.

Critique: These criteria were practical for the rural Indian settings (PHCs and CHCs) where the trial was conducted, as they focused on the most prevalent gestational window for iron intervention.

Blinding and Randomization:
Randomization: Achieved through a computer-generated random permuted block design with allocation concealment via sealed opaque envelopes.

Blinding: The study was "semiblind." Participants and clinicians knew if the treatment was oral or IV, but they were masked to the specific IV formulation used (ferric carboxymaltose vs. ferric derisomaltose). Crucially, outcome evaluators (lab staff and birth assistants) were fully masked to all three arms to prevent bias.

Regulatory and Ethical Compliance

Informed Consent:
The study reports enrollment of participants through standard clinical research procedures, indicating that informed consent was obtained prior to participation. Participants were informed about treatment interventions and monitoring procedures in accordance with ethical research practices.

Ethics Approval:
The trial received oversight from multiple review boards and was performed in compliance with relevant laws and institutional guidelines.

Reporting Standards:
The paper adheres to the CONSORT Statement for transparent reporting of randomized trials.

Data Integrity and Safety

Adverse Events:
Safety monitoring included the evaluation of adverse events such as: Hypophosphatemia Infusion-related reactions Unplanned hospitalizations. Serious adverse events were reviewed by independent physicians and reported to the Data Safety Monitoring Board (DSMB) and the principal investigator. This structured safety monitoring process helps ensure that participant safety is continuously evaluated throughout the trial.

Data Integrity (ALCOA Principles):

  • Attributable: The trial ensured that data could be traced back to specific actions and individuals. For example, Accredited Social Health Activists (ASHAs) were trained to help identify and monitor participants, and all primary outcomes, such as birth weight and hemoglobin levels, were recorded by trained research officers and masked outcome evaluators.
  • Legible: Data recording was standardized. Participants maintained daily medication logs and diaries to track oral iron adherence, and researchers used standardized forms for clinical data at each visit.
  • Contemporaneous: Data were recorded at the time of the event. Hemoglobin and iron indices were assessed at nine predefined visits throughout the pregnancy, and birth weight was strictly measured within 72 hours of delivery.
  • Original: The trial relied on primary source data, such as dating ultrasounds conducted by study-trained sonographers at the time of enrollment and direct laboratory analysis of blood samples at central study hospitals.
  • Accurate: To ensure the data was correct and valid, the researchers verified the quality (iron content and pH) of the IV iron formulations through an external laboratory at the end of the trial. Furthermore, the study used "Multiple Imputation" to statistically handle missing data, ensuring the final results remained as accurate and unbiased as possible.

Potential Monitoring Consideration:
Adherence may be a concern in the oral iron group because oral iron supplementation frequently causes gastrointestinal side effects, which may lead to missed doses and variability in treatment effectiveness compared with single-dose intravenous therapy.

Results and Statistical Interpretation

The trial included a large number of participants distributed across three treatment groups:

  • Oral iron: 1450 participants
  • Ferric derisomaltose: 1456 participants
  • Ferric carboxymaltose: 1462 participants

Primary Outcome 1: Low Birth Weight

  • Oral iron: 29.3%
  • Ferric derisomaltose: 29.1%
  • Ferric carboxymaltose: 25.2%

Statistical analysis showed:

Ferric carboxymaltose vs oral iron
Relative Risk (RR) = 0.87
97.55% Confidence Interval = 0.75–0.99
P value = 0.017

An RR of 0.87 indicates a 13% relative reduction in the risk of low birth weight compared with oral iron therapy. Because the confidence interval does not cross 1 and the p-value is below 0.05, this result is considered statistically significant.

Primary Outcome 2: Maternal Non-Anemic State at Delivery

  • Oral iron: 69.7%
  • Ferric carboxymaltose: RR = 1.05
  • Ferric derisomaltose: RR = 1.06

Confidence intervals:

  • Ferric carboxymaltose: 0.97–1.15
  • Ferric derisomaltose: 0.98–1.16

Because the confidence intervals include 1, the difference was not statistically significant in the primary analysis.

However, sensitivity analyses showed improved outcomes when additional intravenous iron or blood transfusion was not used:

  • Ferric derisomaltose: RR 1.25, P <0.0001
  • Ferric carboxymaltose: RR 1.24, P <0.0001

Conclusion

This randomized clinical trial demonstrates a well-designed multicenter study evaluating treatment strategies for maternal iron deficiency anemia. The large sample size, randomized allocation, and detailed statistical analysis strengthen the reliability of the findings.

The results suggest that single-dose intravenous ferric carboxymaltose significantly reduces the risk of low birth weight compared with oral iron therapy. Although improvements in maternal anemia at delivery were not statistically significant in the primary analysis, additional analyses indicate potential clinical benefits of intravenous iron therapy.

Overall, the study provides valuable clinical evidence supporting the use of intravenous iron formulations as an alternative treatment strategy for moderate iron-deficiency anemia during pregnancy.
